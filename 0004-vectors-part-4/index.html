Vector Dot Product
Multiplying a vector by a vector
Also known as inner product
Performed before + and -
Denoted by $\cdot$
Dot product is the sum of the products of corresponding components
$ \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ . \\ . \\ . \\ a_n \end{smallmatrix} \right] \cdot \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ . \\ . \\ . \\ b_n \end{smallmatrix} \right] = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + ... + a_nb_n$
Dot product Examples
$a\cdot b = \sum_{i=1}^n a_ib_i$
2D: $a\cdot b = a_xb_x + a_yb_y$
3D: $a\cdot b = a_xb_x + a_yb_y + a_zb_z$
Dot product is commutative $a\cdot b = b\cdot a$
$a\cdot b = \sum_{i=1}^n a_ib_i$
$[2\:3]\cdot[4\:-2] = (2)(4) + (3)(-2) = 2$
$[4\:-2]\cdot[2\:3] = (4)(2) + (-2)(3) = 2$
Dot product Geometrically
It is related to the angle between two voctors.
It is equal to the product of the magnitudes of the vectors and the cosine of the angle between the vectors
$a\cdot b = \left\Vert a \right\Vert \left\Vert b \right\Vert cos\:\theta$
Dot product Geometrically
$\theta = a cos \left(\dfrac{a\cdot b} {\left\Vert a \right\Vert \left\Vert b \right\Vert} \right)$
Dot product Geometrically
Note if a and b are unit vectors, we can avoid division. So we just have:
$\theta = a cos \left(a\cdot b\right)$
Dot product Geometrically
If we don't need the exact value, and just the relative orientation of $a$ and $b$, then we just need the sign of the dot product.
Dot product Geometrically
  • $a\cdot b > 0$, $0^{\circ} \leq theta \lt 90^{\circ}$, angle is acute, $a$ and $b$ are pointing the same direction
  • $a\cdot b = 0$, $0^{\circ} \leq theta \lt 90^{\circ}$, angle is right, $a$ and $b$ perpendicular
  • $a\cdot b < 0$, $90^{\circ} \lt theta \leq 180^{\circ}$, angle is obtuse, $a$ and $b$ are pointing the opposite direction
Dot product Geometrically
Note that the if $a$ or $b$ are the zero vector, then it will be zero. So the zero vector is perpendicular to every other vector.
Projecting a vector onto another
With two vectors $v$ and $u$, there exists vectors $v_\Vert$ (or $p$) and $v_\perp$ (or $q$) that are parallel and perpendincular to $u$.
We can compute by using the Dot Product.
Projection
Projection
$v_\Vert = u \frac{\left\Vert v_\Vert \right\Vert} {\left\Vert u \right\Vert}$
Using trig
$cos \theta \left\Vert v \right\Vert = \left\Vert v_\Vert \right\Vert$
Projection
Substiting from slide 5.
$v_\Vert = u \frac{\left\Vert v \right\Vert cos \theta} {\left\Vert u \right\Vert}$
$ = u \frac{\left\Vert v \right\Vert \left\Vert u \right\Vert cos \theta} {\left\Vert u \right\Vert^2}$
$ = u \frac{v\cdot u} {\left\Vert u \right\Vert^2}$
Projection
If $u$ is a unit vector, division is unnecessary, so $v_\Vert$ is an easy solve. On to $v_\perp$
$v_\perp + v_\Vert = v$
$v_\perp = v - v_\Vert$
$ = v - u \frac{v\cdot u} {\left\Vert u \right\Vert^2}$
