Matrices Part 1
What are Matrices
Overview
Rectangular grid of numbers with rows and columns.
Also known as a two-dimensional array of numbers.
Notation
$r\times c$ matrix
$r$ rows by $c$ columns
Notation Example
$2\times 3$
$ \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 2 & 4\\ 2 & 3 & 121\end{smallmatrix} \right] $
Notation Example
$ M = \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} & m_{13} \\ m_{21} & m_{22} & m_{23} \end{smallmatrix} \right] $
Square Matrices
When Rows and Columns are the same number.
$ M = \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} & m_{13} \\ m_{21} & m_{22} & m_{23} \\ m_{31} & m_{32} & m_{33} \end{smallmatrix} \right] $
Diagonal Matrices
When all values are zero, except along the diagonal
$ M = \left[ \begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{smallmatrix} \right] $
A vector is a matrix
Matrices can have any number of rows and columns, including one. Therefore, as we've seen before, both column and row vectors are matrices:
$n\times 1$ and $1\times n$
